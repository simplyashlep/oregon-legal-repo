This letter renews and updates my July 2023 claim and demands payment for damages arising from the City’s six-day dispossession of my homestead vehicle which lacked the property authority, from January 21 through January 27, 2023. While PCC Chapter 16.30 identifies certain immediate consequences of an invalid tow, the obligation to compensate injured parties for consequential losses arises independently under Oregon common law once the City’s control over my property had been conclusively determined to lack such legal authority and is separate from the obligations the City incurs to its towing vendors.\\
\vspace{.5em}
On January 26, 2023, the City’s Hearings Officer determined that the January 21, 2023 tow of my vehicle was \emph{invalid}. That determination is final and binding. Under Portland City Code \textsection 16.30.530(D), the City itself established an exclusive administrative process to adjudicate the validity of vehicle seizures made by the city and made that process final except through judicial writ of review. The City, after fair notice and an opportunity to defend, attended the hearing only by submitting the relevant police reports and, further, elected not to seek judicial review of the Hearings Officer’s ruling. The determination therefore conclusively fixed the tow of my vehicle as an invalid exercise of possession and control over my right to control its use. Once that process yielded a final ruling that the seizure was invalid, the City was foreclosed from re-litigating the legality and standard of its conduct. The character of the City’s dominion over my property during the period of impound was fixed as wrongful, and liability for losses resulting from  that unauthorized dispossession attaches as a matter of law. \\
\vspace{.5em}
The City’s continued refusal to address the consequences of its own final adjudication is untenable and exposes it to avoidable litigation risk. My damages have not diminished since my initial claim notice; they have increased as a result of the City’s prolonged refusal to resolve a claim predicated on already adjudicated illegality. Although the damages likely recoverable through the judicial process would reasonably exceed this amount, I am willing to accept the originally claimed amount plus interest as is designated for liquidated damages under Oregon law, as a compromise settlement to resolve this matter without litigation, if paid by the deadline stated herein. \\

The \$92{,}000 settlement amount reflects an integrated assessment of loss of use of my homestead vehicle, out-of-pocket expenditures incurred during the period of dispossession, and the non-economic effects associated with the deprivation of shelter and personal security during winter conditions, without itemizing categories at this stage. \\

\section{Factual Summary}
\vspace{-.5em}
On January 21, 2023, Portland Police Bureau (PPB) officers initiated a traffic stop as my vehicle was pulling into a designated space at a gas station which was private property. The vehicle remained lawfully parked in that space throughout the encounter and was not obstructing access, blocking fuel pumps, disrupting traffic or creating any safety concern. I was present with my belongings, my dog and advised officers the vehicle served as my home. There is no record that officers contacted the property owner before carrying out the tow, nor was there any record that a signed authorization was obtained.\\
\vspace{.5em}
Officers escalated a simple traffic stop rapidly through multiple uses of force. Unprovoked and unexpectedly, multiple patrol vehicles performed a static box in of my car- going between 3 -5 mph before making contact with my car at the front and rear, with my vehicle at a complete stop. Further, officers employed what the City also considers another use of force in pointing loaded firearms directly at the occupants of my vehicle, including at me through my window, though I was a passenger and had posed no threat nor did officers appear to be concerned with officer safety as we peacefully and patiently waited for the supervisor we'd requested, and they'd agreed to notify. Under the threat of being shot and injured (and possibly killed) I was ordered out, immediately handcuffed, and subjected to a public search including areas under my bra line and between my buttocks in full view of surroundings. We had done nothing to warrant being placed in handcuffs and aggressively handled by officers as they pulled us from my vehicle to begin searching our persons. We presented no signs of fleeing nor being a potential threat to officer safety. Meanwhile, officers searched my vehicle and found nothing incriminating. Although, I remained handcuffed for more than 30 minutes with an officer gripping my arm until I questioned the legal basis for my continued restraint - I couldn't fathom what I'd done to warrant being arrested. Only then did a supervisor direct my release; even so, an officer kept hold of my arm until I insisted on being fully released.\\
\vspace{.5em}
When officers announced they would be towing my vehicle, I stated the vehicle was my home and I had no alternative shelter. It was January in Portland, with temperatures in the mid-30s to low-40s. Although it was not required based on my vehicle being lawfully parked on private property, I offered to have someone with a valid license move the vehicle, consistent with PPB policies allowing alternatives to impoundment. That offer was rejected. My partner was arrested and transported to jail. Despite the forseeable risks of harm that are likely to result from depriving someone of their sole means of shelter, I was left on the street in an unfamiliar area that officers deisgnated as a "high-vice" area at night,  with my belongings and dog.\\
\vspace{.5em}
Over the following six days, the loss of my homestead vehicle converted existing homelessness into acute displacement. The vehicle was my only stable shelter and my base of daily functioning. Without it, I lacked protection from the elements, privacy, or independent access to my belongings. During this period, I alternated between sleeping on my daughter’s bedroom floor at her father’s apartment, riding public transit to remain in populated and well-lit areas, sleeping outdoors, and, when financially possible, staying in budget hotels.

I relied exclusively on public transportation, often making hours-long trips to reach the tow lot, City offices, and other necessary locations. I spent substantial time researching Portland City Code and PPB directives and preparing for the tow hearing while simultaneously attempting to meet basic survival needs without access to most of my personal property, which remained in the vehicle.\\
\vspace{0.5em}
On February 16, 2023, the Code Hearings Officer issued a Notification of Invalid Tow and final Order concluding that, although certain technical criteria were cited, the tow was “nevertheless unjustified” and violated both PPB policy and City Code requirements.

The Order emphasized that I owned the vehicle, was present and occupying it as my dwelling at the time of the stop, and offered a licensed driver to move it. The record contained no evidence of unlawful parking or any legitimate safety concern—only an officer-initiated escalation following the inaccurate characterization of my passengers and me as “sovereign citizens” for calmly asserting our rights. The Hearings Officer found that PPB policy instructing officers to avoid impounding vehicles used as dwellings was disregarded, that the tow served no community-caretaking function, and that it was imposed punitively in response to perceived noncooperation. The Order directed that I bear no towing or storage charges.\\
\vspace{.5em}
After prevailing at the tow hearing, I obtained the required release paperwork and coordinated a pickup time with the tow company. A friend and I traveled nearly two hours by bus to the tow yard and arrived at the agreed time to find the gate closed and no one present. For approximately four hours, while waiting in cold and dark conditions, dispatch repeatedly answered my calls and stated that a driver would arrive shortly.

Ultimately, when I placed another call, a dispatcher answered and stated words to the effect of, “You think you’re getting that car back for free—think again,” before disconnecting the call. Only after I contacted the City and requested intervention did the tow company unlock the gate and release my vehicle—six days after the tow. \\

\section{Legal Framework: Binding Effect of the Hearings Officer’s Quasi-Judicial Determination}

This demand does not ask the City to concede liability for an unadjudicated tort. The legality of the January 21, 2023 seizure of my vehicle—and the responsibility that flows from that determination—was adjudicated through the City’s own quasi-judicial administrative process under Portland City Code (“PCC”) \S 16.30.530.

On January 26, 2023, Hearings Officer William Guzman issued a written determination finding the tow \textbf{“INVALID.”} PCC \S 16.30.530(D) expressly provides that \textbf{“[t]he decision of the hearings officer is final.”} Under Oregon law, where a municipal code establishes an adjudicative process that applies preexisting legal criteria to concrete facts, affords notice and an opportunity to be heard, and declares the resulting decision final, that determination is quasi-judicial and binding unless timely challenged through the exclusive mechanism of writ of review. \textit{Nelson v. Emerald People’s Utility Dist.}, 318 Or 99, 106, 862 P2d 1293 (1993); ORS 34.020(1).\\
\vspace{0.5em}

The January 26, 2023 determination satisfies each of these criteria. The Hearings Officer applied the tow-validity standards set forth in PCC Chapter 16.30 to a discrete tow event involving identified parties, following an adversarial process that resulted in written findings and a written order. No writ of review was sought. The statutory period for judicial review expired in approximately March 2023. The determination therefore became final and unreviewable.\\
\vspace{0.5em}

By enacting PCC Chapter 16.30, the City both authorized vehicle seizures and created an exclusive administrative mechanism to adjudicate the legality of those seizures and assign responsibility when a tow is declared invalid. Having structured its authority in this manner, the City is bound by the outcomes of that process. Once the Hearings Officer issued a final determination that the seizure lacked legal authority, the City’s own adjudicative framework fixed the character of the seizure as unauthorized and foreclosed post hoc justification. The Code identifies certain immediate financial consequences of an invalid tow, but it does not purport to exhaust the legal consequences that follow from an officially determined lack of authority.\\
\vspace{0.5em}

At this post-adjudication stage, Risk Management’s role is administrative rather than evaluative. The City’s adjudicative process resolved the only question Risk Management lacks authority to revisit: whether the City was legally entitled to deprive me of possession and use of my vehicle during the period of impoundment. Any refusal to give effect to that final determination would place the City in the position of disregarding a binding adjudication issued pursuant to a process the City created, declared final, and chose not to challenge through the sole available mechanism of judicial review.\\
\vspace{0.5em}

If the City forces litigation, it will not be defending an unresolved tort claim. It will be explaining why it declined to honor the legal effect of its own final quasi-judicial determination and the obligations that attach to an officially determined lack of authority.


\subsection{The Hearings Officer’s Factual Findings Would Withstand Judicial Review}

Even had the City timely sought a writ of review, a reviewing court would apply the substantial evidence standard under ORS 34.040(2). In writ of review proceedings, courts do not ``determine the facts anew.'' Rather, they assess whether the decision-making body’s findings are supported by substantial evidence—evidence that a reasonable person could rely upon to support the conclusion reached. \textit{Younger v. City of Portland}, 305 Or 346, 752 P2d 262 (1988). The court does not reweigh evidence or substitute its judgment for that of the administrative decision-maker.\\
\vspace{0.5em}

The Hearings Officer’s findings are amply supported by substantial evidence in the record, including: my undisputed testimony that I was present in my own vehicle and offered to arrange for a licensed driver to move it; the Hearings Officer’s express finding that ``there is no evidence in the record that [Claimant] was `verbally aggressive' as described in the police report''; evidence within the police reports themselves showing that the driver used a turn signal, undermining the stated basis for the traffic stop; and the finding that ``there is no evidence that Claimant knew the driver did not have a valid driver’s license.''\\
\vspace{0.5em}

Under these deferential review standards, the Hearings Officer’s determination would be upheld. Risk Management’s assertion that there was ``no evidence of negligence'' is legally irrelevant and cannot displace factual findings made by a quasi-judicial officer applying the governing Code standards. Any attempt to override those findings inverts the proper standard of review and improperly substitutes an executive agency’s judgment for binding quasi-judicial factfinding.\\

\subsection{Issue Preclusion Bars Relitigation of Tow Validity}

Under Oregon’s collateral estoppel doctrine, the Hearings Officer’s determination that the tow was “nevertheless unjustified” constitutes a binding factual determination that may not be relitigated in subsequent proceedings. Issue preclusion applies when: (1) the issue was actually litigated and decided; (2) the determination was essential to the final decision; (3) the party against whom preclusion is asserted had a full and fair opportunity to be heard; and (4) the same issue is presented in a subsequent proceeding. \textit{Powell v. Witherspoon}, 241 Or App 410, 251 P3d 141 (2011).\\
\vspace{0.5em}

Each element is satisfied. The identical issue—whether the January 21, 2023 tow complied with PCC Chapter 16.30—was actually litigated and necessarily decided. The City had notice of the hearing, the opportunity to appear, and the right to present evidence and cross-examine witnesses, whether or not it elected to exercise those rights. The Hearings Officer determined, based on sworn testimony and documentary evidence, that the tow was unjustified—a finding essential to the Order directing that I bear no towing or storage charges. That determination is final under PCC §§ 16.30.450(A) and 16.30.530(D) and is binding in subsequent proceedings.\\

\subsection{Basis Asserted for Initial Stop}
Officers identified two grounds as to the basis for the stop: (1) asserted lack of visible license plates or permit, and (2) asserted failure to signal before turning into the gas station lot. Both were addressed administratively.\\
\vspace{0.5em}
The Hearings Officer expressly found officers' account regarding absence of a visible trip permit was not credible and that the vehicle displayed a lawfully issued and affixed permit. That finding necessarily reflects officers either observed the permit and disregarded it or failed to conduct adequate observation before initiating the stop.\\
\vspace{0.5em}
Oregon law distinguishes between turns from one street to another at an intersection and movements from public roadway onto private property. In \textit{State v. Bea}, 318 Or 220, 228, 864 P2d 854 (1993), the Oregon Supreme Court interpreted ORS 811.335 and ORS 811.400 and concluded that the signaling requirement applies to ``a change in direction of travel made from one street to another at the intersection of two or more streets.'' Here, the vehicle entered a private gas station parking lot from SE Powell Boulevard—a movement from public roadway onto private property rather than from one street to another.\\
\vspace{0.5em}
ORS 810.410(3) authorizes a traffic stop when an officer has reasonable cause to believe a traffic offense has been committed. The administrative record reflects each stated ground was at best tenuous and, as to the permit, affirmatively contradicted by the Hearings Officer's findings. As to the signal, the Oregon Supreme Court undeniably would be the authority on the matter and has established that a lack of signal into the gas station station parking lot would not constitute a failure to signal for the purpose of authorizing a traffic stop. \\

\subsection{Legal Framework Governing Tow Location}	
The Hearings Officer evaluated the tow under PCC Chapter 16.30, which addresses tows from public roadways. The vehicle, however, was parked in a designated space on private property when officers ordered the tow. Portland City Code separately regulates tows from private property through PCC Chapter 7.24, imposing requirements distinct from those applicable to public roadway tows.\\
\vspace{0.5em}
PCC 7.24.050(A)--(B) addresses private property impounds and requires express written authorization from the property owner or lawful possessor, as well as contemporaneous contact with that owner or agent. The administrative record contains no indication the property owner or authorized representative was contacted or that written authorization was obtained before the tow.\\
\vspace{0.5em}
The City routed this matter through PCC Chapter 16.30's tow-hearing process and obtained a final determination appealable only by way of writ of review under that framework. The City chose not to appear at that hearing. It presented no witnesses, offered no sworn testimony, and subjected no officers to cross-examination. The Officer's findings rests entirely on the City's own written reports and my testimony. Under established administrative law principles, the City cannot now collaterally attack or relitigate a final quasi-judicial determination it elected not to contest but had a full and fair opportunity to be heard. The quasi-judicial finding that the tow was ``nevertheless unjustified'' is a binding factual and legal determination made by the City's designee within the City's own adjudicative scheme after sworn testimony and a full evidentiary hearing. \\
\vspace{0.5em}
The tow hearing officer’s decision constitutes a quasi-judicial administrative determination: it applied pre-existing legal criteria to a discrete set of adjudicative facts, resolved the rights and obligations of identified parties, and resulted in a final order directed to a specific factual circumstance. Decisions of this type are subject to judicial review only by writ of review under ORS 34.010 et seq., and when writ of review is available, it is the exclusive remedy for challenging the decision. No writ was sought. The determination therefore stands as final and binding.\\
\vspace{0.5em}
Under Oregon law, final quasi-judicial administrative determinations are entitled to preclusive effect. Issue preclusion applies where (1) the issue is identical and was actually litigated and essential to a prior final decision on the merits; (2) the party to be precluded had a full and fair opportunity to be heard and is the same party or in privity with a party to the prior proceeding; and (3) the prior proceeding is the type to which courts give preclusive effect. Nelson v. Emerald People’s Utility Dist., 318 Or 99, 862 P2d 1293 (1993). Each element is satisfied here. The City was the responding party in the administrative proceeding, the legality of the tow was directly litigated, and the invalidity determination was essential to the final order.\\
\vspace{0.5em}
Accordingly, the City may not now relitigate, recharacterize, or disregard that determination under the guise of “risk assessment” or discretionary claims handling. Where the operative facts have been actually litigated and resolved, and where the claim could have been raised in the earlier proceeding, subsequent relitigation is barred. Colhouer v. Union Pac. R.R., 275 Or 559, 551 P2d 1291 (1976).\\
\vspace{0.5em}
Critically, the payment obligation at issue does not arise from a discretionary tort settlement framework. It flows directly from the City Code and the final administrative determination issued under it. Risk Management’s role at this stage is purely ministerial: to implement the consequences of the City’s own adjudicative process, not to reopen liability, reassess fault, or condition payment on litigation leverage.\\
\vspace{0.5em}
Forcing litigation under these circumstances would require the City to reconcile—and attempt to justify—its refusal to honor a binding quasi-judicial determination rendered pursuant to its own established procedures. That posture would place the City in the position of disregarding the finality and legal effect of its own administrative adjudications, contrary to settled Oregon law governing writ review, administrative preclusion, and municipal decision-making.\\

\section {Legal Basis for Liability}
Although the Hearing Officer’s findings reflect that the officers’ conduct failed to meet required standards of care, this demand is not premised on negligence liability. Negligence concepts such as foreseeability or proximate cause are therefore not elements the City must evaluate or relitigate. Liability here arises from a final quasi-judicial determination that the tow was unauthorized and invalid, and from the mandatory legal consequences that Oregon law and City Code attach to that status.

\subsection{The Payment Obligation Arises From City Code, Not Tort Law}
The obligation to pay charges following an invalid tow determination arises directly from Portland City Code Section 16.30.530(C), which mandates: \textbf{``The City of Portland shall be responsible for all towing and/or storage charges and other allowable charges accruing until [the specified date and time].''} This is a Code-imposed obligation, not a tort damage award subject to proof of negligence. The Hearings Officer did not ``award damages'' in the traditional sense---the Officer determined the tow was invalid and applied the automatic consequence specified in City Code: City payment responsibility for all charges incurred.\\

The word ``shall'' in PCC Section 16.30.530(C) is mandatory, not discretionary. Under established principles of statutory construction, ``shall'' imposes a duty. \textit{Lakin v. Senco Products, Inc.}, 329 Or 62, 987 P2d 463 (1999). When City Code states the City ``shall be responsible'' for charges following an invalid tow determination, no discretion exists. The obligation is automatic and mandatory.\\
\vspace{0.5em}
Risk Management's role is not to decide whether the City will pay---City Code has already made that decision. Risk Management's role is to implement the Code's directive by processing payment. Any assertion that Risk Management may ``deny'' payment based on its own evaluation of ``negligence'' contradicts the plain language of the Code and exceeds Risk Management's authority.

\subsection{Wrongful Detention of Homestead Property}
\vspace{-.5em}
The City's six-day detention of my vehicle without lawful justification constitutes wrongful interference with personal property under Oregon law. The Oregon Supreme Court adopted Restatement (Second) of Torts § 222A's six-factor test for determining whether interference is sufficiently serious to require the actor to pay the chattel's full value (\textit{Mustola v. Toddy}, 253 Or 658, 663, 456 P2d 1004 (1969).\\
\vspace{-2em}:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item extent and duration of actor's exercise of dominion or control;
\item actor's intent to assert a right inconsistent with the other's right of control;
\item actor's good faith;
\item extent and duration of resulting interference with owner's right of control;
\item harm done to the chattel; and
\item inconvenience and expense caused to the other.
\end{enumerate}

The Hearings Officer's final decision under PCC 16.30.450(A) supplies the dispositive factual predicate: the tow was ``nevertheless unjustified,'' served no legitimate community-caretaking purpose, and was imposed as punishment though officers knew the vehicle was my home.

Under Oregon property law, an owner wrongfully deprived of possession is entitled to compensatory damages measured by the value of use during deprivation, together with all losses naturally and foreseeably flowing from that wrongful detention. See \textit{Hoffman v. Brinton}, 59 Or 11, 22 (1911) (recognizing recovery for loss of use and consequential harm); \textit{Atl. Richfield Co. v. Farm Credit Servs.}, 226 F3d 1138, 1154 (10th Cir 2000) (applying Oregon law to allow loss-of-use and reasonably foreseeable consequential damages). Where property serves as a dwelling, those damages include loss of shelter, stability, dignity, and access to essential belongings.

Because the Hearings Officer's final decision under PCC 16.30.450(A) supplies factual findings that the City's six-day detention was unjustified, the deprivation constitutes wrongful taking and detention of personal property within the meaning of ORS 12.080(4), which carries a six-year limitations period. The Officer's findings that officers knew the vehicle was my home and abandoned me at night underscore that resulting harms were foreseeable and within the scope of risk the tow created.

\subsubsection{Collateral Estoppel Effect of Administrative Determination}
\vspace{-.5em}
Under Oregon's collateral estoppel doctrine, the Hearings Officer's determination that the tow was ``nevertheless unjustified'' constitutes a binding factual finding precluding relitigation in subsequent proceedings. Issue preclusion applies when: (1) the issue was actually litigated and decided; (2) the determination was necessary to the judgment; (3) the party had full and fair opportunity to be heard; and (4) the same issue is presented in a subsequent proceeding. \textit{Powell v. Witherspoon}, 241 Or App 410, 251 P3d 141 (2011).

All elements are satisfied. The City had notice of the hearing, opportunity to appear, right to cross-examine witnesses, and right to present evidence. It exercised none of these rights. The Hearings Officer determined, based on sworn testimony and documentary evidence, that the tow was unjustified—a finding necessary to the Order directing that I bear no charges. That determination is final under PCC 16.30.450(A) and binding for all purposes.

\subsubsection{Statutory Liability Under PCC 16.30.530(C)}

PCC 16.30.530(C) provides: ``If the charges are owed to a private company, the City will pay them if, after a hearing, the tow is found to be invalid or for any other reason not justified and the charges have not previously been paid.''

This language creates a mandatory, nondiscretionary municipal payment obligation triggered when a tow is found ``invalid or for any other reason not justified.'' The ordinance both shifts responsibility for tow charges and codifies that an unjustified tow violates City law.

The City's failure to satisfy this obligation constitutes a liability created by statute actionable under ORS 12.080(1)--(2)'s six-year period.

\textbf{PCC 16.30.530(C) Does Not Displace Common-Law Remedies}

Nothing in PCC 16.30.530 contains ``exclusive remedy'' language or purports to displace other relief. Under Oregon statutory construction canons, express remedies are presumed cumulative unless the legislature clearly manifests exclusivity intent. ORS 174.010; \textit{Strawn v. Farmers Ins. Co.}, 350 Or 336, 258 P3d 1199 (2011). PCC 16.30.530(C) establishes a floor—minimum reimbursement—not a ceiling on damages recoverable under common law for wrongful detention.

Moreover, PCC Chapter 16.30 creates an administrative tow-challenge process, not a comprehensive tort-damages scheme. Section 16.30.530(C) addresses only who pays the tow company—it does not purport to eliminate common-law remedies for wrongful interference with property. The Oregon Supreme Court distinguishes between administrative procedures (which may be exclusive for their specific purpose) and independent tort liability. \textit{Holman v. State of Oregon}, 358 Or 190, 362 P3d 182 (2015).

Oregon law supplies the measure of consequential damages in the absence of express limitation: when a statute imposes a mandatory duty, failure to perform that duty gives rise to an action under ORS 12.080(1) for resulting damages, including foreseeable consequential harm.

The Hearings Officer's determination that the tow was ``nevertheless unjustified'' both triggers the City's payment duty under PCC 16.30.530(C) and confirms the tow was unlawful under City standards, supporting recovery of consequential damages.

\subsubsection{Inapplicability of Negligence Analysis}

Wrongful detention of personal property is an intentional interference claim, not a negligence claim, and does not depend on any contract between the parties. Once intentional dominion is shown, a defendant's mistake, good faith, or due care is ordinarily immaterial.

The question is whether the City intentionally ordered and maintained a six-day detention that deprived me of my vehicle and home, not whether it did so carefully or ``without negligence.'' Risk Management's October 11, 2023 denial asserting ``no evidence of negligence by the City of Portland'' is legally beside the point and cannot override the Hearings Officer's binding determination for three independent reasons.

A post hoc claims investigation cannot lawfully supersede a final quasi-judicial determination; the City must conform its risk and litigation posture to established record and findings.

Second, City policy treats unjustified uses of authority—including unjustified impounds—as disciplinable conduct outside the scope of authorized action. The City cannot treat ``unjustified'' as grounds for officer discipline while denying that an unjustified impound triggers compensable liability for the person whose home and property were wrongfully taken. That posture would nullify the tow-hearing process and insulate the City from accountability under its own Code.

Third, Oregon Tort Claims Act notice requirements have long been satisfied by my 2023 claim filing. ORS 12.080—not shorter personal-injury periods—provides the governing limitations framework for liabilities created by statute and for taking or detaining personal property.

\subsection{Negligence Per Se From Statutory Violations}
\vspace{-.5em}
The City's October 11, 2023 denial asserting "no evidence of negligence" reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of Oregon negligence law. Under Oregon law, "the failure to obey the requirements of a law or statute which, for safety or protection of others, commands or requires certain acts or conduct, or forbids or prohibits certain acts or conduct, is negligence per se; or, in other words, negligence in and of itself, regardless of what an ordinary and prudent person might do in the absence of such law, the law having established what is required." \textit{Basko v. Sterling Drug, Inc.}, 416 F2d 417, 426 (2d Cir 1969) (applying Oregon law).

Negligence per se requires no analysis of reasonableness or care. It flows automatically from violating a statute or regulation designed to protect a class of persons when that violation proximately causes harm to a member of the protected class.
\vspace{-.5em}
\subsubsection{Multiple Statutory Violations Established by Administrative Record}

The Hearings Officer's findings establish multiple violations of mandatory safety provisions:

\textbf{First}, if the tow is evaluated under PCC Chapter 16.30 (public roadway tows), the Hearings Officer expressly found that "PPB policy directing officers to avoid impounding vehicles used as dwellings had not been followed." That policy exists to protect homeless individuals from losing their shelter through unnecessary impounds. Officers knew the vehicle was my home, knew I had a licensed alternative driver available, and towed anyway. That violation of mandatory policy constitutes negligence per se.

\textbf{Second}, if the tow is properly evaluated under PCC 7.24.050(A)--(B) (private property tows), the administrative record contains no evidence officers obtained the required express written authorization from the property owner or made contemporaneous contact with that owner or agent. Those requirements exist to protect vehicle owners from arbitrary towing from private property. The City's failure to comply with PCC 7.24's mandatory preconditions constitutes negligence per se.

\textbf{Third}, the Hearings Officer found the tow was imposed "as punishment for perceived lack of cooperation" rather than for any legitimate community-caretaking purpose. PPB directives prohibit punitive use of impound authority. That violation constitutes negligence per se.

Each violation satisfies Oregon's negligence per se framework: (1) the violated provision was designed to protect a class of persons; (2) I am a member of that class; (3) officers violated the provision; and (4) the violation proximately caused my harm. \textit{Fazzolari v. Portland School Dist. No. 1J}, 303 Or 1, 17, 734 P2d 1326 (1987).

\subsubsection{The City Cannot Violate Safety Statutes Without Negligence}

The City faces a logical impossibility: it cannot simultaneously maintain that officers violated mandatory safety requirements (as the Hearings Officer found) and claim no negligence occurred. Violation of a mandatory safety statute or regulation \textit{is} negligence—that is the meaning of negligence per se. The City's "no negligence" position is legally incoherent and cannot survive the administrative record.

Moreover, the question is not whether officers acted carefully or in good faith while violating mandatory requirements. Negligence per se liability attaches regardless of the actor's subjective intent or efforts at compliance. The law established what was required; officers failed to comply; harm foreseeably resulted. That is the definition of negligence per se under Oregon law.\\

\item \textbf{Secure Storage of Possessions (\$25/day)}: Storage unit rates in Portland range roughly from \$50--150 per month (\$1.60--5.00 per day) for basic storage; the higher daily value accounts for 24/7 access and integration with shelter and transportation in a single secure space.

\item \textbf{Pet Shelter (\$15/day)}: Pet-friendly motel surcharges and kennel costs typically fall in the \$10--50/day range; the vehicle provided unconditional pet accommodation without breed, weight, or behavioral restrictions.

\item \textbf{Privacy and Dignity (\$25/day)}: Oregon law recognizes that housing-related deprivations can cause compensable dignitary and privacy harms, particularly when a person is effectively forced from independent living to sleeping on a floor without privacy or control. The valuation reflects that loss of autonomy rather than a separate speculative category of non-economic damages.
\end{itemize}

These amounts cover only the six days of actual deprivation. They exclude losses from foregone opportunities, extensive legal-research time, and health impacts, which are addressed separately under consequential and emotional-distress damages.

Interest on liquidated amounts is calculated under ORS 82.010(1)(a), which sets a default rate of nine percent per annum (simple interest) on moneys after they become due. Those amounts were ascertainable on February 16, 2023, when the Hearings Officer issued the final Order.

\item Annual interest: \$2,400.00 $\times$ 0.09 = \$216.00
